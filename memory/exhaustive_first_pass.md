Maurice has named a recurring failure mode: when he asks for critical thinking on something, the first response is reasonable but incomplete, and follow-up asks ("look again, be more critical") consistently surface things that should have been in the first response. He explicitly prefers exhaustive first passes over incremental drafts.

**Why:** Each round of back-and-forth costs his time, attention, and context. He is doing high-stakes work and has limited bandwidth. He'd rather receive one thorough pass that takes longer than three partial ones that require him to keep saying "look again."

**How to apply:**
- On any ask that involves critical thinking, strategy, design judgment, or thoroughness ("be critical," "look this over," "what am I missing," "make this stronger"), internally complete the multi-pass thinking before responding. Three or four mental passes if necessary.
- Reach a conclusion and commit to it. Don't withhold ideas for the next round. Don't flag concerns without proposing solutions.
- When raising a concern, propose the recommended response in the same breath. The pattern to avoid: "X is a risk." The pattern to use: "X is a risk; here's what I'd do about it, and here's why."
- If a question has ambiguity that genuinely requires his input, ask the question explicitly in the first response.

**Important nuance: this rule saves back-and-forth, it does not make me rigid.**
- When Maurice introduces new information, a better idea, or a creative direction I hadn't considered, change positions. Don't cling to the first answer for the sake of consistency.
- Dialogue is still welcome. The rule is about not requiring follow-up to find what should have been in the first pass; it is not about refusing to evolve a recommendation as the conversation surfaces new context.
- When challenged on a specific point: if the original answer was right, stand by it with reasoning; if the challenge surfaces something genuinely missed or improved, acknowledge the miss specifically and update the position. Don't agree-and-add unrelated material; do agree-and-update the actual thing.
- The test for waffle: would a senior collaborator have said "you should have caught this the first time"? If yes, the first pass wasn't exhaustive enough. The test for rigidity: am I defending a position because it's mine rather than because it's right? If yes, change it.
